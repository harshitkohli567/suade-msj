# Analysis

Before drafting, read `output-and-context-contract.md` for the output format, citation rules,
and how to use the style profile and any already-completed sections. This file covers what's
specific to drafting the Analysis section.

This is the core of the motion — where the undisputed facts, measured against the governing law,
are shown to require judgment for the moving party. It depends more heavily on other sections
than any other part of the MSJ: it should use the Legal Standards section as its rule bank and
the Statement of Undisputed Material Facts as its fact bank, introducing no new law and no new
facts of its own. If either of those sections hasn't been drafted yet for this matter, say so
plainly in working notes — an Analysis built without them is working from the underlying
documents directly and is at real risk of drifting from the rules and facts the rest of the
motion actually rests on.

## Steps

**1. Pin down the relief sought.** Confirm exactly what the motion asks the Court to do —
judgment on all claims, selected claims, discrete elements, particular damages or remedies, or
an affirmative defense — using the Statement of Claim(s) if it exists. Address only the claims,
defenses, or issues the motion actually presents.

**2. Build an element-by-element map before writing anything.** For each claim the motion
addresses, lay out a working table: claim, legal element, governing rule (from Legal Standards),
supporting undisputed fact(s) (from the SUMF, by paragraph number), and the result — does the
fact establish the element, negate it, show the nonmoving party lacks evidence for it, establish
an affirmative defense, or show the claim is legally insufficient? Don't start drafting prose
until every claim has been mapped this way; it's what keeps the Analysis disciplined instead of
argumentative.

**3. Apply the law to the facts, one argument at a time.** For every argument: state the
governing rule (from Legal Standards), identify the undisputed material fact, cite its SUMF
paragraph, explain why that fact satisfies, negates, or defeats the element, and conclude that
judgment is warranted as a matter of law on that point. Introduce no legal standard that Legal
Standards omitted, and no fact that the SUMF omitted — if you find yourself needing either, that's
a signal the upstream section needs revisiting, not that this section should quietly patch the
gap.

**4. Show why no trial-worthy dispute exists.** For each claim, explain concretely why the
nonmoving party can't create a genuine issue for trial — because their position rests on
speculation, conclusory allegations, immaterial disagreements, unsupported assertions,
inadmissible evidence, mere legal disagreement, or a credibility question the Court doesn't need
to resolve. Make it clear the case can be decided on this record without a hearing or trial.

**5. Address adverse facts directly.** Go back to the material adverse facts the SUMF surfaced,
and for each one, either distinguish it, explain why it's immaterial, show why it doesn't create
a genuine dispute, show why it doesn't satisfy a required element, or address it under the
specific claim or defense it bears on. Don't skip a fact opposing counsel is likely to use — a
Court that senses the record wasn't fairly confronted trusts the rest of the motion less.

**6. Organize by claim or issue, in Legal Standards's order.** A common structure: the plaintiff
can't establish Claim 1; the plaintiff can't establish Claim 2; the undisputed facts establish
Defendant's affirmative defense; the plaintiff can't recover a particular category of damages.
Head each subsection with the legal conclusion you're asking the Court to reach — "The undisputed
facts establish that Plaintiff did not suffer an adverse employment action," not a bare label
like "Retaliation Claim."

**7. Keep the summary judgment posture correct throughout.** Argue from the moving party's
perspective, but never ask the Court to weigh evidence, choose between competing testimony,
decide credibility, or draw a contested inference in the moving party's favor. Instead, argue
that even under the proper standard, the nonmoving party lacks evidence sufficient to support an
essential element or defeat entitlement to judgment.

**8. Draft with a consistent rule → fact → consequence → conclusion structure.** Each analytical
unit: state the legal requirement, identify the undisputed facts, explain the legal consequence,
address any adverse fact, and conclude that summary judgment should enter on that claim or issue.
Keep the tone direct and judicial — the force here comes from disciplined application of law to
undisputed fact, not from argument for its own sake.

## Checklist

Before finalizing, confirm the Analysis:

- Addresses every claim the motion presents, element by element.
- Follows the same order as Legal Standards.
- Ties every factual assertion to a specific SUMF paragraph.
- Introduces no material fact and no legal rule for the first time here.
- Explains, for every argument, why the fact is legally material — not just what it says.
- Addresses every material adverse fact, or consciously explains why it's excluded as immaterial.
- Never asks the Court to weigh evidence or resolve credibility.
- Distinguishes immaterial factual disputes from genuine disputes of material fact.
- Shows plainly why no hearing or trial is necessary.
- Ends each subsection with a clear conclusion that judgment should enter on that claim, defense,
  issue, or remedy.
- Supports the Introduction's core proposition: no disputed material fact or credibility question
  requires trial, and the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.

## Domain knowledge

The Analysis is where the MSJ proves itself — everything before it sets the table. In a
Massachusetts employment MSJ, it typically turns on whether the plaintiff can establish the
elements of the claim: protected status, protected activity, adverse employment action,
causation, pretext, discriminatory animus, failure to accommodate, unpaid wages, contractual
entitlement, damages, or timely exhaustion, depending on the claim.

For discrimination and retaliation claims, be careful with burden-shifting: identify where the
burden sits, what the plaintiff must show at that stage, and why the undisputed record prevents
them from meeting it. If the employer has articulated a legitimate, non-discriminatory or
non-retaliatory reason, explain why the undisputed facts show the plaintiff can't establish
pretext. For wage and compensation claims, focus on statutory entitlement, payment timing,
compensation classification, individual liability where it applies, and whether the record shows
payment or non-entitlement as a matter of law. For contract-based claims, focus on formation, the
specific obligation breached, performance, breach, causation, and damages — don't treat workplace
unfairness as legally sufficient unless the claim itself recognizes it.

Stay faithful to the summary judgment posture throughout: the Court may decide legal consequences
from undisputed facts, but it may not resolve genuine factual disputes or decide credibility. The
strongest MSJ Analysis never says "the employer's witnesses are more credible" — it says "even
accepting the properly supported record under the governing standard, the plaintiff lacks
evidence of an essential element."

## Best practices

- Build the element-by-element analysis map before drafting a single sentence of prose.
- Treat Legal Standards as the rule bank and the SUMF as the fact bank — don't reach past either.
- Lead each subsection with the conclusion the Court should reach.
- Explain why each fact matters legally, not just what it says.
- Avoid factual storytelling — this section applies law, it doesn't narrate.
- Avoid emotional rhetoric or attacks on the opposing party.
- Address adverse facts before opposing counsel can weaponize them.
- Treat immaterial disputes as immaterial, but never pretend a material dispute is undisputed.
- Rely on credibility only to say that no credibility determination is necessary.
- Aim for inevitability: rule, undisputed fact, legal consequence, judgment — each step following
  cleanly from the last.
