# Statement of Undisputed Material Facts

Before drafting, read `output-and-context-contract.md` for the output format, citation rules,
and how to use the style profile and any already-completed sections. This file covers what's
specific to drafting the Statement of Undisputed Material Facts (SUMF).

This section is the evidentiary foundation the rest of the motion is built on — not a narrative
of the case, and not a place to persuade. Its only job is to present, in numbered paragraphs,
the material facts the admissible record establishes without genuine dispute. The Analysis
section will cite these paragraphs by number, so precision and verifiability here matter more
than anywhere else in the motion.

If a Statement of Claim(s) already exists for this matter, use it to scope which facts are
material — a fact matters here only if it bears on an element, defense, or issue that section
identifies as actually before the Court. If Legal Standards has also been drafted, its stated
elements are a second, more granular check on materiality.

## Steps

**1. Map the material issues.** From the claims, defenses, and legal issues actually before the
Court, list every legal element and every factual proposition that could establish or negate it.
This map is what tells you, later, which facts are worth including — a fact that doesn't connect
to an element doesn't belong here no matter how interesting it is.

**2. Review the complete evidentiary record.** Go through depositions, affidavits, declarations,
interrogatory responses, admissions, personnel and payroll records, correspondence, agreements,
evaluations, disciplinary records, ESI, and exhibits in full. Don't sample — a fact you didn't
check against the whole record can't reliably be called undisputed.

**3. Extract material facts as neutral propositions.** Each extracted fact should state one
proposition, phrased neutrally, with a pinpoint citation to its supporting document. Build this
complete fact record before you start deciding what belongs in the final Statement — drafting and
selecting are separate steps, not one motion.

**4. Test materiality.** For each candidate fact, ask whether it establishes or negates an
element, establishes a defense, establishes required timing or notice, establishes an undisputed
employment action, establishes relevant damages, or is necessary context for another material
fact. Exclude anything that's background or narrative without bearing on the legal analysis.

**5. Test whether each fact is genuinely undisputed.** For every candidate, look at all
supporting evidence and the opposing party's evidence, and determine whether competent evidence
creates a genuine factual dispute — as distinct from a legal disagreement or an unsupported
denial. A fact isn't undisputed just because the opposing party denies it; it's undisputed only
if the record wouldn't let a reasonable factfinder conclude otherwise.

**6. Check for contradictions across the record.** Before treating anything as settled, compare
all evidence bearing on the same proposition. Confirm the statements actually concern the same
actor, event, time period, and scope before assuming a conflict — and don't resolve genuine
conflicts yourself. If competing admissible evidence creates a real dispute, that proposition
comes out of the Statement, full stop.

**7. Surface material adverse facts.** Look specifically for facts unfavorable to the moving
party: would leaving them out mislead the Court, would opposing counsel rely on them, do they
weaken a key proposition or support an alternative explanation? Genuinely undisputed adverse
facts belong in the Statement even though they're unhelpful — omitting them doesn't make them go
away, it just means the Analysis section has to handle them without warning.

**8. Organize logically.** Arrange surviving facts starting from foundational employment facts,
generally chronologically, grouping related facts for clarity, with one principal proposition
per numbered paragraph. The goal is that the Court can follow the record without reconstructing
it from the exhibits itself.

**9. Verify every citation before drafting.** For each surviving fact, confirm admissible
evidence actually supports it, the citation correctly identifies the source, quotations are
accurate, and the cited material supports the specific proposition it's attached to. If a
sentence can't be traced to the record, it doesn't survive.

**10. Draft.** Write each numbered paragraph as one neutral, non-argumentative proposition with a
precise citation — no legal conclusions, no characterization, no persuasion. State only what the
evidence shows; reserve everything else for Analysis.

## Checklist

Before finalizing, confirm the Statement:

- Every fact is material to a legal element, defense, or issue actually before the Court.
- Every fact is genuinely undisputed, not merely asserted as such.
- Every factual assertion is supported by admissible evidence with an accurate pinpoint citation.
- No proposition requires the Court to resolve credibility or weigh competing evidence.
- Every material adverse fact has been considered and, where genuinely undisputed, included.
- Apparent contradictions were checked across the full record before drafting.
- Immaterial background facts were excluded.
- Facts are organized logically, one principal proposition per numbered paragraph.
- The record here will actually support every argument the Analysis section needs to make.

## Domain knowledge

The Statement of Undisputed Material Facts is not a factual background section — it doesn't tell
the story of the case, it establishes the record the Court can rely on to decide the motion. A
fact is material only if it could affect the disposition of a claim or defense under the
governing law; background, however interesting, doesn't belong here unless it helps the Court
understand a material issue.

A fact is undisputed only where the record wouldn't permit a reasonable factfinder to conclude
otherwise — the Court doesn't resolve credibility, weigh competing evidence, or choose between
reasonable inferences at summary judgment, so facts that would require any of those don't belong
in this section as "undisputed."

This is a build-then-select discipline: extract broadly from the full record, test relevance,
check for contradictions and adverse facts, verify support, and only then draft. That order is
what keeps the Statement reliable rather than merely convenient.

## Best practices

- Build the full factual record before drafting the Statement itself.
- Include only facts that materially bear on the Court's legal analysis.
- State facts objectively and let the evidence carry the weight, not the phrasing.
- One principal proposition per numbered paragraph, precisely cited.
- Test every proposed fact for materiality before it makes the cut.
- Review the entire record for contradictions before calling anything undisputed.
- Weigh whether omitting a material adverse fact would mislead the Court, and include it if so.
- Exclude legal conclusions, argumentative language, speculation, and credibility assessments —
  every one of those belongs in Analysis, not here.
- Make sure the finished Statement gives Legal Standards and Analysis a complete, reliable
  foundation to build on without either section needing to go back to the exhibits itself.
