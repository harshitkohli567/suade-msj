# Legal Standards

Before drafting, read `output-and-context-contract.md` for the output format, citation rules,
and how to use the style profile and any already-completed sections. This file covers what's
specific to drafting the Legal Standards section.

This section's only job is to hand the Court the rules it needs — not to apply them, not to
argue from them, just to state them clearly and cite them accurately. A reader should finish
this section knowing the legal framework the motion operates in, with no idea yet how the motion
comes out. That restraint is what makes the section useful to a judge, and it's also what makes
the later Analysis section clean: every rule it applies should already be sitting here, stated
once and correctly.

If a Statement of Claim(s) already exists for this matter, treat its claim inventory and naming
as fixed — state standards only for the claims and defenses it identifies as within the motion's
scope, in the same order, using the same names. That ordering and naming will carry straight
through to Analysis.

## Steps

**1. Identify the legal issues actually before the Court.** Work from the operative complaint,
answer, affirmative defenses, and the motion's own scope (as established in the Statement of
Claim(s), if drafted) to list every claim and defense the MSJ addresses, and confirm which
Massachusetts forum's law governs. Include only what's necessary to decide this motion — a
Legal Standards section padded with irrelevant law makes the Analysis section harder to follow.

**2. State the summary judgment standard first.** Lay out that summary judgment is appropriate
where there's no genuine dispute of material fact and the moving party is entitled to judgment
as a matter of law; that the nonmoving party can't rest on allegations, speculation, or
conclusory assertions; and that the Court doesn't weigh credibility or resolve factual disputes
at this stage. This sets up the theme the rest of the motion runs on: no trial is needed because
no genuine factual dispute exists.

**3. Extract the elements of each claim.** For each employment claim — discrimination under
Chapter 151B, retaliation, hostile work environment, failure to accommodate, wage-and-hour,
breach of contract, wrongful termination, defamation, intentional interference, unpaid
commissions, or whatever the matter involves — identify its statutory or common-law source, its
required elements, the plaintiff's burden, any burden-shifting framework, and any exhaustion,
timeliness, or administrative prerequisites. Stay purely at the rule level here; the facts come
later.

**4. Work from the top of the authority hierarchy down.** Prioritize Massachusetts statutes,
then Supreme Judicial Court decisions, then Appeals Court decisions, then federal decisions
applying Massachusetts law, and only reach for persuasive authority if controlling law is
genuinely unavailable or unclear. Don't import general employment-law propositions where
Massachusetts-specific law actually governs — that's a common and avoidable weakness in a
Massachusetts MSJ.

**5. Extract rules, not case facts.** Pull out elements, burdens, definitions, statutory
requirements, recognized defenses, and standards for things like causation, pretext, protected
activity, adverse action, comparators, damages, or limitations periods. Include precedent facts
only when necessary to explain the rule itself, and don't analogize the record to precedent here
— that belongs to Analysis, not this section.

**6. Organize by claim, in Analysis's order.** Typical structure: the summary judgment standard,
then a subsection per claim in the same sequence the Analysis section will follow, then
affirmative defenses or damages issues if relevant. Each subsection should hand the Court exactly
the rule it needs before Analysis applies it.

**7. Confirm every rule is still good law.** Before including anything, verify the case hasn't
been overruled or limited, the statute hasn't been amended, the cited language still means what
you're using it for, the authority applies in the relevant forum, and it actually supports the
proposition you're citing it for. An outdated or distinguishable citation undermines the whole
section's credibility.

**8. Draft.** Write clean, neutral, rule-focused prose: one legal rule or closely related set of
rules per paragraph, cited to controlling authority, with no factual argument, no rhetorical
language, and no application of the rule to this case's record. The reader should come away
knowing the framework, not the outcome.

## Checklist

Before finalizing, confirm the Legal Standards section:

- States the summary judgment standard accurately.
- Covers only claims actually addressed by the motion (per the Statement of Claim(s), if drafted).
- States each claim's elements completely and correctly.
- Uses Massachusetts authority wherever Massachusetts law governs.
- Includes burden-shifting rules where they apply.
- Includes exhaustion or limitations requirements where relevant.
- Contains no outdated, overruled, or misstated legal standard.
- Contains no factual argument and no analogizing of case facts to precedent.
- Supports every legal proposition with authority.
- Is organized in the same order the Analysis section will follow.
- States exactly the rules that Analysis will go on to apply — no more, no less.

## Domain knowledge

This section supplies the legal framework the Court needs to decide the motion — it persuades
through precision, not through fact application. In a Massachusetts employment MSJ, that
typically means two layers: the summary judgment standard itself, and the substantive
employment-law standards for each claim actually at issue.

For discrimination and retaliation claims, check whether a burden-shifting framework applies and
state it clearly. For wage or compensation claims, check statutory definitions, timing
requirements, damages provisions, and any individual-liability rules. For contract-based
employment claims, identify formation, breach, performance, and damages elements distinctly.

Stay precise, current, and restrained — don't hide weak law, don't overstate favorable authority,
and don't import out-of-jurisdiction standards where Massachusetts law controls. A judge reading
this section should trust it as a fair statement of the law, because that trust is what makes the
Analysis section persuasive when it applies these same rules to the undisputed facts.

## Best practices

- State the summary judgment standard before any claim-specific law.
- Keep this section rule-focused; leave fact application to Analysis entirely.
- Use controlling Massachusetts authority wherever it exists.
- Mirror the Analysis section's order exactly.
- State each claim's elements precisely enough that Analysis can walk through them one by one.
- Include burden-shifting frameworks only where they genuinely apply.
- Include limitations, exhaustion, causation, damages, or remedy standards only when relevant to
  this motion.
- Skip long block quotes unless the exact statutory or judicial language matters.
- Don't summarize precedent facts for their own sake, and don't argue that the facts here satisfy
  or fail a standard — that's Analysis's job.
- Keep it clean and useful to the judge: clear rule, clear authority, no clutter.
- Make sure every rule stated here is one the Analysis section actually applies — and that
  Analysis introduces no legal standard that isn't already sitting in this section.
