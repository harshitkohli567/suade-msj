# Conclusion

Before drafting, read `output-and-context-contract.md` for the output format, citation rules,
and how to use the style profile and any already-completed sections. This file covers what's
specific to drafting the Conclusion.

The Conclusion isn't a separate argument — it's where the motion states its requested disposition
clearly and closes with precision. Everything it says should already have been established
earlier in the motion; its only job is to say, briefly and exactly, what the Court should now do.

This section leans on prior sections more than any other except Analysis: the relief it requests
must match the Introduction's framing, the claim names must match the Statement of Claim(s), and
the scope of relief requested must match what the Analysis section actually supports — never
broader. If the Analysis section hasn't been drafted yet, flag that clearly in working notes,
since a Conclusion written without it risks requesting relief the record hasn't actually earned.

## Steps

**1. Identify the requested relief precisely.** Determine whether the motion seeks judgment on
all claims, judgment on selected claims, partial judgment on discrete issues, dismissal of
specific damages or remedies, or judgment on an affirmative defense. The Conclusion must match
the actual relief requested in the motion — no more, no less.

**2. Reconfirm the core basis, without rearguing it.** Return briefly to the central proposition:
no genuine dispute of material fact, no credibility determination needed, no trial required to
resolve the claims addressed, and entitlement to judgment as a matter of law. The Conclusion
reinforces this; it doesn't relitigate it.

**3. Tie the relief to the claims addressed.** Check the Conclusion against the Analysis section
claim by claim — for each claim or issue Analysis addressed, determine what the Conclusion should
request for it (judgment for the defendant, judgment for the plaintiff on liability, dismissal of
a count, dismissal or limitation of damages, or another specific order). Never request relief
that Analysis didn't actually support.

**4. Draft a concise closing.** Write a short final paragraph that refers back to the undisputed
record, states that the governing law requires judgment, names the party in whose favor judgment
should enter, and asks the Court to allow the Motion for Summary Judgment. Keep it direct and
restrained — no new facts, no new law, no new argument belongs here.

## Checklist

Before finalizing, confirm the Conclusion:

- Clearly requests that the Court grant the Motion for Summary Judgment.
- Identifies the party seeking judgment.
- Matches the relief actually requested in the motion — nothing broader than what Analysis
  supports.
- States that no genuine dispute of material fact exists, and, where appropriate, that no
  credibility determination or hearing is required.
- Introduces no new facts, no new legal authorities, and doesn't repeat the full Analysis.
- Is fully consistent with the Introduction, the Statement of Claim(s), and the Analysis.
- Is concise, formal, and court-facing.

## Domain knowledge

The Conclusion's function is narrow by design: state the requested disposition clearly and close
the motion with precision, not argue anything new. In a Massachusetts employment MSJ, it should
generally state that the undisputed material facts show the plaintiff can't establish one or more
required elements, or that the employer (or plaintiff, if the motion runs the other way) is
otherwise entitled to judgment as a matter of law.

Where the motion is brought by an employer-defendant, request judgment for the defendant on the
specified claims. Where the motion is partial, be explicit about which claims, issues, damages
theories, or remedies are covered — vagueness here undercuts an otherwise precise motion.

Preserve the summary judgment framing to the very end: don't suggest the Court is choosing the
moving party's version of contested facts, but emphasize that the properly supported record
presents no genuine dispute requiring trial and no credibility issue requiring a hearing. The
strongest Conclusion makes the requested order feel inevitable, because the facts, the law, and
the Analysis already did the work — this section just says so.

## Best practices

- Keep it brief — this is the shortest section in the motion for a reason.
- Don't restate the factual background or repeat the full legal analysis.
- Introduce no new authority and no new facts.
- Use the same claim names used throughout the motion.
- Match the scope of requested relief exactly to what Analysis supports.
- Use formal language appropriate to a Massachusetts court filing.
- Close with a clear, direct request that the Court allow the motion — a formulation like "For
  these reasons, and because the undisputed material facts establish that no trial-worthy issue
  exists, the Court should allow [Party]'s Motion for Summary Judgment" is a reliable pattern.
