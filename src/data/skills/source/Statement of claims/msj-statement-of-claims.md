# Statement of Claim(s)

Before drafting, read `output-and-context-contract.md` for the output format, citation rules,
and how to use the style profile and any already-completed sections. This file covers what's
specific to drafting the Statement of Claim(s).

This section exists to tell the Court exactly what claims exist and which of them the motion is
about — nothing more. It is procedural and descriptive, not argumentative. A reader should
finish it knowing precisely what's pending, who asserted what against whom, and what's actually
in scope for this motion, without having formed any view yet on whether those claims survive.

If an Introduction has already been drafted for this matter, the claims and relief described here
must match it exactly — don't introduce a claim or scope the Introduction didn't anticipate, and
flag it in working notes if the underlying documents suggest the Introduction's framing needs
revisiting. Because Legal Standards and Analysis will both use the claim names and ordering set
here as their own scaffolding, get the naming and scope right the first time — later sections
will follow whatever this one establishes.

## Steps

**1. Establish the operative pleading.** Identify the complaint or amended complaint that
currently governs the action, who asserts each claim, against whom, and which claims (if any)
have already been dismissed, withdrawn, or otherwise resolved. Everything downstream depends on
working from the live pleading, not an earlier or superseded version.

**2. Inventory every cause of action.** For each claim in the operative pleading, capture its
legal theory, its statutory or common-law source, its essential elements, and whether it's
pleaded independently or in the alternative. Use the characterization the pleadings themselves
give the claim — this isn't the place to recharacterize or paraphrase it into something cleaner.

**3. Scope the motion.** Determine precisely which claims, counts, discrete issues, or damages
theories the MSJ actually addresses, and just as importantly, name what's explicitly outside its
scope. A Statement of Claims that's vague about scope makes the rest of the motion harder to
follow and easier to challenge procedurally.

**4. Confirm procedural posture.** Note any amendments, prior dispositive rulings, voluntary
dismissals, stipulations, or previously resolved claims that affect what's actually before the
Court now. Only describe developments that bear on the claims currently live.

**5. Cross-check against the record.** Compare your claim inventory against the operative
complaint, any answer, counterclaims, court orders, and docket entries, and resolve any
inconsistency before drafting. Never describe a claim that is no longer pending — that's an easy,
credibility-costing error to make.

**6. Draft.** Write a concise procedural summary: the operative pleading, each claim relevant to
the motion, its governing legal basis, which claims the motion addresses, and the current
procedural posture. Don't summarize evidence, argue why a claim fails, or touch disputed facts —
all of that belongs to Legal Standards and Analysis, which will build directly on the claim
inventory established here.

## Checklist

Before finalizing, confirm the Statement of Claim(s):

- Identifies the operative complaint or amended complaint.
- Correctly inventories every pending cause of action, with its statutory or common-law basis.
- Correctly identifies who asserts and who defends each claim.
- States plainly whether the motion addresses all claims or only specific ones.
- Reflects the current procedural posture accurately, and omits claims already dismissed or
  resolved.
- Uses terminology consistent with the pleadings themselves.
- Contains no factual argument, no merits evaluation, and no unnecessary evidence citation.
- Is fully consistent with the Introduction (if drafted) and sets up the Legal Standards and
  Analysis sections cleanly.

## Domain knowledge

An MSJ is decided claim by claim, and each cause of action carries its own elements, burdens, and
governing authority. The Statement of Claim(s) exists to define that landscape precisely as
pleaded, and to mark which parts of it the motion actually reaches.

In Massachusetts employment litigation, claims commonly arise under Mass. Gen. Laws ch. 151B
(discrimination and retaliation), the Massachusetts Wage Act, the Equal Pay Act, or common-law
theories like breach of contract, promissory estoppel, wrongful termination, or defamation.
Identify the correct governing basis for each without evaluating whether its elements are
satisfied — that evaluation is what the rest of the motion is for.

This section provides the framework the rest of the motion runs on: the Statement of Undisputed
Material Facts supplies the record, Legal Standards supplies the rules, and Analysis applies
those rules to that record for each claim named here. If a claim is mislabeled or mis-scoped
here, that error will propagate through every later section.

## Best practices

- Name claims exactly as pleaded — don't clean up or simplify the pleadings' own language.
- Clearly separate pending claims from dismissed, withdrawn, or resolved ones.
- State the governing statutory or common-law basis for each claim where it applies.
- Describe only what's relevant to the motion; leave out claims outside its scope.
- Present the procedural posture objectively — this section persuades no one, it informs.
- Avoid any argumentative characterization of the claims or the parties.
- Leave out evidence summaries and disputed facts entirely.
- Don't evaluate whether a claim's elements are satisfied here.
- Use the exact same claim names throughout the motion — Legal Standards and Analysis will
  inherit whatever naming convention is set here, so consistency matters more than usual.
- Keep it concise and procedural: a clear roadmap of what's before the Court, not an argument
  about how it should come out.
