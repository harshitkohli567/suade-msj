# Introduction

Before drafting, read `output-and-context-contract.md` for the output format, citation rules,
and how to use the style profile and any already-completed sections. This file covers what's
specific to drafting the Introduction.

The Introduction's job is narrow: orient the Court, state the legal conclusion, and make clear
that the motion presents a legal question, not a factual dispute requiring trial. It is not the
place to prove anything — that's what the rest of the motion is for. A reader who only read the
Introduction should understand exactly what's being asked and why, without yet seeing the
supporting detail.

If a Statement of Claim(s), Statement of Undisputed Material Facts, or Analysis section already
exists for this matter, treat their claim names, party references, and legal theory as settled —
don't re-derive them from the underlying documents. If none exist yet (the Introduction is often
drafted first), work directly from the case documents and flag in working notes that later
sections should stay consistent with the framing chosen here.

## Steps

**1. Pin down what the motion actually asks for.** Identify the moving party, and whether
judgment is sought on all claims, specific claims, discrete elements, particular damages or
remedies, or an affirmative defense. Everything else in the Introduction follows from this — an
Introduction that overstates the relief sought undermines the whole motion.

**2. Confirm there's no genuine dispute of material fact.** For each material element, check
that the supporting evidence isn't genuinely contradicted by admissible opposing evidence.
Separate factual disagreements (which can defeat summary judgment) from legal disagreements
(which can't), and set aside disputes over facts that don't actually affect the outcome. Don't
call something undisputed unless the record actually supports that.

**3. Confirm no credibility determination is required.** Summary judgment is inappropriate if
deciding the motion means choosing between competing witness accounts, weighing conflicting
evidence, or drawing a contested inference in the moving party's favor. Only proceed once you've
confirmed the material facts rest on evidence that doesn't require the Court to decide whom to
believe.

**4. Identify the controlling legal theory.** Note the governing Massachusetts statutes and
controlling Massachusetts appellate authority (plus federal authority applying Massachusetts law
where relevant), and confirm that applying that law to the undisputed facts entitles the moving
party to judgment. You're identifying the theory here, not arguing it — full legal analysis
belongs in Legal Standards and Analysis.

**5. Distill it to one core proposition.** The Introduction should read as a single throughline:
what the case is about, why the material facts are undisputed, why no credibility call is needed,
and why the law requires judgment for the moving party. Present this as an objective conclusion
that follows from the record, not as advocacy for its own sake.

**6. Draft.** Write a concise section that identifies the motion, briefly frames the dispute,
states plainly that no genuine dispute of material fact exists and no credibility determination
is required, states that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law, and closes
by requesting that the Court grant the motion. Leave the detailed factual and legal argument to
the sections built for it — an Introduction that tries to win the case on its own usually reads
as overreaching.

## Checklist

Before finalizing, confirm the Introduction:

- Clearly identifies the moving party and the claims addressed by the motion.
- Accurately describes the procedural posture.
- States that no genuine dispute of material fact exists, and that no credibility determination
  is necessary.
- States that the undisputed facts entitle the moving party to judgment as a matter of law.
- Doesn't overstate the evidentiary record or cite authority inaccurately.
- Is consistent with any Statement of Undisputed Material Facts or Analysis already drafted for
  this matter.
- Introduces no facts unsupported by the record, and no argument that isn't developed later in
  the motion.
- Ends with a clear request that the Court grant the Motion for Summary Judgment.

## Domain knowledge

A Motion for Summary Judgment asks the Court to decide a case, or discrete claims, without trial
because the record shows no genuine dispute as to any material fact and the moving party is
entitled to judgment as a matter of law. The Introduction orients the Court and previews why —
it doesn't need to prove every element itself.

A fact is material only if it could affect the outcome under the governing law; disputes over
immaterial facts don't block summary judgment. A dispute is genuine only if the evidence would
let a reasonable factfinder rule for the nonmoving party. And summary judgment is out of reach
if resolving it requires weighing competing evidence, assessing credibility, or picking between
reasonable inferences — so the Introduction should frame the case as presenting legal questions
on an undisputed record, not factual ones needing a jury.

In Massachusetts employment cases specifically, name the governing claims accurately —
discrimination, retaliation, wage-and-hour, breach of contract, wrongful termination, and so on
— without arguing their merits here; that argument belongs in Legal Standards and Analysis.

## Best practices

- Lead with the legal conclusion, not the factual background — the Court should know the ask
  within the first sentence or two.
- Keep the focus on what's material to summary judgment; everything else is noise here.
- Frame the dispute as a legal question resolvable on the existing record.
- Avoid rhetoric, emotional language, or attacks on opposing witnesses — the force of an MSJ
  Introduction comes from precision, not intensity.
- Never characterize disputed evidence as undisputed, and never lean on credibility to get to
  judgment.
- Trace every factual assertion directly to admissible record evidence.
- Stay fully consistent with the Statement of Undisputed Material Facts and Analysis, whether
  they're already drafted or will be drafted after this section.
- Use precise terminology — "material" dispute and "immaterial" disagreement are not
  interchangeable, and the Court will notice if they're conflated.
- Keep it concise and roadmap-oriented. Detailed argument belongs later; this section's job is
  to make the reader want to keep reading with the right expectations set.
